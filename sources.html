Sources and Legal Foundations

Languages: EN · FR · ES · DE · IT · PT · NL · PL · RU · ZH · AR

This petition is grounded in internationally recognized legal instruments, United Nations treaties, and established legal doctrine relating to public debt, democratic consent, and fundamental human rights.

It does not rely on a single national case, political position, or economic model. Its purpose is to articulate a general normative framework applicable across jurisdictions and institutional contexts.

I. Purpose and Scope of This Document

This document identifies the legal and normative sources that inform the petition.

It does not constitute:

  • a legal judgment,
  • a judicial decision,
  • a formal accusation against specific actors.

Its function is to clarify the existing legal principles on which the petition is based and to ensure transparency regarding its foundations.

II. International Legal Instruments

1. Charter of the United Nations (1945)

The petition is grounded in the principles enshrined in the Charter of the United Nations, in particular:

  • the primacy of human rights,
  • the obligation of international cooperation,
  • the commitment to promote social progress and improved standards of living.

These principles establish that financial and economic obligations cannot override fundamental human rights commitments undertaken by States.

2. International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights (1966)

The petition draws directly on the International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights, notably:

  • the obligation to progressively realize economic, social, and cultural rights,
  • the prohibition of unjustified retrogressive measures,
  • State responsibility for access to health, education, housing, work, and social protection.

The relationship between public debt policies and social rights is central to this covenant and to the petition’s analytical framework.

3. Right of Peoples to Self-Determination

As recognized in Article 1 common to the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights and the International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights, the right of peoples to self-determination underpins sections of the petition addressing:

  • the absence of effective democratic consent,
  • the burden imposed on present and future generations by long-term public debt obligations.

III. General Principles of Human Rights Law

4. Principle of Non-Regression

The principle of non-regression, developed through international jurisprudence and the practice of United Nations treaty bodies, holds that:

States should not adopt policies that lead to a durable deterioration of fundamental rights without compelling and proportionate justification.

This principle directly informs the petition’s analysis of austerity measures and debt-servicing policies.

5. State Obligations and Proportionality

International human rights law requires States to:

  • assess the foreseeable impact of economic policies on fundamental rights,
  • ensure proportionality between policy objectives and social harm,
  • prioritize the protection of vulnerable populations.

These obligations are relevant when evaluating debt-related fiscal measures.

IV. Legal Doctrine on Illegitimate and Odious Debt

6. Odious Debt Doctrine (Alexander Sack, 1927)

The petition is structurally aligned with the doctrine of odious debt, formulated by Alexander Sack, which identifies three cumulative criteria:

  1. absence of genuine public consent,
  2. absence of benefit for the population,
  3. creditor awareness of these conditions.

These criteria are used as an analytical framework, without asserting automatic legal invalidity of any specific debt.

7. Contemporary Legal and Institutional Practice

The petition reflects reasoning developed in:

  • international legal scholarship,
  • parliamentary and independent debt audit processes,
  • reports and analyses by international and regional institutions.

The text is deliberately abstract in order to remain applicable across different legal and political systems.

V. Methodological Clarifications and Limits

This petition:

  • does not declare any specific debt illegal or void,
  • does not replace judicial or arbitration mechanisms,
  • does not constitute a refusal of international cooperation or solidarity.

It constitutes a reference document, intended to:

  • support analysis and debate,
  • inform institutional, academic, and civil society initiatives,
  • contribute to policy and legal reflection at national and international levels.

Conclusion

This petition is anchored in existing international law and recognized legal doctrine. It seeks to reaffirm the principle that human rights obligations must prevail over illegitimate financial constraints, in accordance with the commitments already undertaken by States under international law.
