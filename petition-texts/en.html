International Petition

Reference document – Version 1.1

Preamble

We, citizens and residents, taxpayers and persons affected by public debt policies, considering that:

  • States have a primary obligation to guarantee fundamental human rights;
  • These obligations prevail over financial constraints;
  • Certain public debts were contracted without genuine democratic consent, without benefit to the populations, and with creditors’ knowledge.

We wish to draw the attention of national and international institutions to the structural and ongoing violations of human rights resulting from illegitimate public debt repayment policies.

I. Legal foundations

This petition is based in particular on:

  • The Charter of the United Nations;
  • The International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights;
  • The principle of peoples’ right to self-determination;
  • The obligation of non-regression in fundamental rights;
  • The shared responsibility of public and private actors in human rights violations.

II. Findings

In many countries, public debt servicing leads to lasting cuts in health, education, housing and social protection budgets, imposed austerity policies, deteriorating living conditions, and violations of dignity and economic security.

III. Absence of democratic consent

Many debts were contracted without explicit popular mandate or informed democratic debate, and their consequences are borne by current and future generations without consent.

IV. Absence of benefit for populations

A significant portion of these debts did not serve essential infrastructure or social rights, but instead benefited private interests or refinanced previous debts without democratic control.

V. Creditors’ awareness

Institutional and private creditors could not ignore the non-democratic nature of certain decisions and the foreseeable human rights impacts of repayment policies.

VI. Our demands

  • Independent, transparent and public debt audits;
  • Suspension of repayments that violate fundamental rights;
  • Recognition of illegitimate or odious debts;
  • Protection of populations against economic retaliation;
  • Referral to international human rights mechanisms.

Conclusion

No people should be forced to renounce their fundamental rights in order to repay debts contracted against their interests.
